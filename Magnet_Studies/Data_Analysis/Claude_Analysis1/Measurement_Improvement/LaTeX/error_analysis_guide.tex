\section{Overview and Context}
\label{sec:overview}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{What Are We Measuring?}

This experiment measures whether radiation in the CEBAF accelerator tunnel at
Jefferson Lab causes permanent magnets to lose their magnetization. This matters
because the proposed FFA (Fixed Field Alternating-gradient) accelerator upgrade
would use permanent magnets extensively, and they need to survive the tunnel
radiation environment for decades.

\subsection{The Physical Setup}

We deployed 30 ``Y-plates'' in the CEBAF tunnel at 8 different locations for
approximately 12 months (January 2025 to January 2026). Each Y-plate holds
exactly 4 permanent magnets, one of each grade:

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Material grades deployed on each Y-plate.}
\label{tab:materials}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
Material & Family & Why it matters \\
\midrule
\textbf{N42EH}  & NdFeB (neodymium)       & Strong, radiation-sensitive, contains 1--2\% dysprosium \\
\textbf{N52SH}  & NdFeB (neodymium)       & Strongest grade, radiation-sensitive, 0\% dysprosium \\
\textbf{SmCo33H}& SmCo (samarium cobalt)  & Radiation-hard control, lower field \\
\textbf{SmCo35} & SmCo (samarium cobalt)  & Radiation-hard control, lower field \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The 4 material slots on each plate are randomized (not always in the same order)
across 4 cyclic patterns (P1 through P4, with 8, 8, 7, and 7 plates each).

An additional 9 Y-plates are kept in the lab as unexposed controls:
Y-\{8,\,14,\,27,\,28,\,29,\,31,\,33,\,35,\,37\}. These never see tunnel
radiation and serve as our ``null experiment.''

There are also H-plates (Halbach-style assemblies, 4 slots each in
Alpha/Beta/Gamma/Delta configurations) and A-samples (small paired magnets).
The Y-plates are the primary discovery measurement because of the gain-immune
differential technique. However, the H-plates and A-samples are critical for the
broader science story: H-plates directly demonstrate degradation behavior in
Halbach field geometries (the actual FFA use case), and A-samples provide paired
field-vs-zero-field comparisons. For high-impact publications, demonstrating
consistent results across all three sample types, using both integrated moment
(Helmholtz) and spatially-resolved point field (Teslameter) measurements, is the
goal.

\subsection{What ``Percent Change'' Means}

For every magnet sample, we measure its magnetic moment before deployment (the
``baseline'') and after deployment. The percent change is:

\begin{equation*}
\Delta_i = \frac{H_{\mathrm{latest}} - H_{\mathrm{baseline}}}{H_{\mathrm{baseline}}} \times 100\%
\end{equation*}

where $H$ is the Helmholtz coil reading in milliWeber-centimeters (mWC),
corrected to a common reference temperature of \SI{20}{\degreeCelsius}.

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Negative $\Delta$} = demagnetization (lost field). This is what
    we expect from radiation damage.
  \item \textbf{Positive $\Delta$} = apparent gain. Usually noise or a
    measurement artifact, but can indicate thermal aging recovery in SmCo.
  \item \textbf{Zero $\Delta$} = no change, within noise.
\end{itemize}

Our headline result: NdFeB magnets lost ${\sim}0.17$--$0.25\%$ of their field
after 12 months in the tunnel. SmCo magnets showed no statistically significant
change. The difference between these (the ``differential'') is $-0.208\%$,
detected at $7.6\sigma$ statistical significance
(Figure~\ref{fig:material-comparison}).

\section{Instruments}
\label{sec:instruments}
%=============================================================================

We use two complementary instruments. They measure different physical quantities
and have very different noise characteristics.

\subsection{Helmholtz Coil + Fluxmeter (Model 2130)}
\label{sec:helmholtz}

\textbf{What it measures:} The total magnetic moment of a sample by integrating
the flux change when the sample is inserted into a calibrated Helmholtz coil
pair.

\textbf{Output units:} milliWeber-centimeters (mWC), proportional to the
sample's total dipole moment.

\textbf{Key specifications:}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item DC accuracy: 0.050\% typical, 0.100\% maximum (of full scale)
  \item 12 ranges: \num{100}\,kMT to \num{100}\,MMT, with $4\tfrac{3}{4}$ digit resolution
  \item Auto drift compensation ($\pm 1\%$ of range)
  \item Position-independent: reading is ``fairly independent of sample position
    within the coils'' as long as the sample is approximately centered
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Why we use it:} The Helmholtz measurement integrates over the entire
sample volume, so it gives a true total-moment measurement that is insensitive
to exactly where the magnet sits inside the coil. This is crucial because our
samples are hand-inserted.

\textbf{The big catch: gain drift.} The fluxmeter output drifts by
0.5--0.7\% from session to session. This is NOT the instrument's intrinsic
accuracy (which is 0.05\%); it comes from drifting coil parameters, cable
connections, and electronics temperature. This drift is the single largest
systematic error for any individual magnet measurement. The intra-plate
differential technique (Section~\ref{sec:differential}) eliminates it.

\textbf{Data file format:} Tab-separated \texttt{.dat} files with columns:

\begin{lstlisting}
date    time    dosimeter_id    rod_id    value unit
2026-06-02  09:21:19  XA01080034Q  R-825  0.9123 mWC
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Teslameter (SENIS 3MH6-E) + Hall Probe Type C}
\label{sec:teslameter}

\textbf{What it measures:} The magnetic field vector at a single point on the
magnet surface, using a 3-axis CMOS Hall sensor.

\textbf{Output units:} milliTesla (mT) for each axis ($B_x$, $B_y$, $B_z$).
The magnitude $|B| = \sqrt{B_x^2 + B_y^2 + B_z^2}$ is also computed.

\textbf{Key specifications:}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item DC accuracy: 0.01\% (\SI{100}{ppm}) at $\pm$\SI{0.1}{\tesla}/\SI{0.5}{\tesla}/\SI{2}{\tesla} ranges
  \item Temperature stability: $<$\SI{20}{ppm/\degreeCelsius}
  \item Resolution: ${\sim}$\SI{1}{\micro\tesla} RMS at \SI{10}{SPS} (${\sim}$\SI{0.5}{ppm})
  \item 3-axis CMOS Hall probe, sensing volume ${\sim}100 \times 10 \times \SI{100}{\micro\meter\cubed}$
  \item Built-in temperature sensor (reads probe junction temperature)
  \item Calibration stored in probe EEPROM; maintains \SI{100}{ppm} on probe swap
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Why we use it:} Two reasons:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Temperature:} The built-in probe temperature is our primary
    temperature measurement for the Helmholtz temperature correction
    (Section~\ref{sec:tempcorr}).
  \item \textbf{Independent cross-check:} Field measurements on three faces
    (top, front, side) provide an independent check on demagnetization trends.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{The big catch: positioning noise.} The Teslameter measures field at a
single point. If the sample sits \SI{0.1}{\milli\meter} differently in the
3D-printed rig, the field at the probe location changes. This ``positioning
noise'' dominates the Teslameter error budget (0.14--1.90\% depending on face;
see Section~\ref{sec:positioning}).

\textbf{Data file format:} Tab-separated \texttt{.dat} files:

\begin{lstlisting}
date    time    dosimeter_id    rod_id    Bx    By    Bz    temp_C
2026-06-02  09:24:59  XA01080034Q  R-825  -180.053  -4.250  -3.566  26.708
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Arduino AM2302 Temperature/Humidity Sensor (NEW, June 2026)}
\label{sec:arduino}

\textbf{What it measures:} Ambient air temperature and relative humidity near
the Helmholtz coil setup, via an ASAIR AM2302 (DHT22-equivalent) digital sensor
connected to an Arduino.

\textbf{Output units:} Temperature in degrees Celsius, relative humidity in
percent.

\textbf{Key specifications:}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Temperature accuracy: $\pm$\SI{0.5}{\degreeCelsius}
  \item Temperature resolution: \SI{0.1}{\degreeCelsius}
  \item Response time: ${\sim}$\SI{2}{\second} (thermal time constant)
  \item Humidity accuracy: $\pm$2--3\% RH
  \item Humidity range: 0--100\% RH
  \item Update interval: ${\sim}$\SI{2}{\second}
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Why we added it:} The Teslameter's temperature reading comes from the
Hall probe junction, which is pressed against (or near) the magnet surface. This
means the ``temperature'' it reports is influenced by the magnet's temperature
(which may lag behind room temperature) and possibly by Joule heating in the
probe itself. The AM2302 measures ambient air temperature near the setup, which
is an independent data point.

\textbf{Important caveat:} Neither sensor directly measures the sample's true
internal temperature, which is what the temperature correction formula actually
needs. The Teslameter probe is in thermal contact with the sample surface, so it
may be closer to the sample temperature; the Arduino sensor measures the
surrounding air, which could differ from the sample temperature if the sample
hasn't fully equilibrated, or could be influenced by drafts, laptop fans, or
HVAC airflow near the sensor. Which sensor provides the better proxy for the
temperature correction is an open question that the cross-calibration study
(Priority 2 in the summer plan) is designed to answer.

\textbf{June 2026 data shows} the Teslameter reads 0--\SI{2.7}{\degreeCelsius}
higher than the Arduino at low temperatures, with the discrepancy shrinking to
near zero at high temps. Possible explanations include probe self-heating,
thermal lag in the magnet (the sample may genuinely be warmer than the air early
in the day), or sensitivity of the Arduino to local air currents. Disentangling
these effects is a key goal of the summer cross-calibration work.

\textbf{Data file format:} Tab-separated \texttt{*\_helmholtzTemp.dat} files:

\begin{lstlisting}
date    time    dosimeter_id    rod_id    temp degC    humidity %
2026-06-02  09:21:19  XA01080034Q  R-825  25.80 degC  42.30 %
\end{lstlisting}

Each helmholtzTemp file corresponds to one Helmholtz measurement of one sample.
The Arduino reading is taken at the moment of the Helmholtz measurement, while
the Teslameter reading may be taken minutes later (when the sample is moved to
the Teslameter rig). There are 484 helmholtzTemp files from the June 2--3
measurement campaign.

\section{Temperature Correction}
\label{sec:tempcorr}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Why Temperature Correction Matters}

All permanent magnets get weaker when they warm up and stronger when they cool
down. This is a reversible, well-understood thermodynamic effect described by the
temperature coefficient of remanence, $\alpha(B_r)$, expressed in percent per
degree Celsius.

If we do not correct for temperature, a magnet measured at \SI{28}{\degreeCelsius}
will appear to have lost about 0.8\% of its field compared to a baseline taken at
\SI{20}{\degreeCelsius}, even if no radiation damage occurred. Since our signal is
only ${\sim}0.2\%$, temperature correction is essential.

\subsection{The Formula}

All readings are corrected to a common reference temperature:

\begin{equation*}
H_{\mathrm{corrected}} = \frac{H_{\mathrm{raw}}}{1 + \alpha \,(T_{\mathrm{measured}} - T_{\mathrm{ref}})}
\end{equation*}

where:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item $H_{\mathrm{raw}}$ = the raw Helmholtz reading in mWC
  \item $\alpha$ = temperature coefficient for this material (fractional, e.g.\ $-0.0010$)
  \item $T_{\mathrm{measured}}$ = temperature at the time of measurement, in \si{\degreeCelsius}
  \item $T_{\mathrm{ref}} = \SI{20.0}{\degreeCelsius}$ (our reference temperature)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Temperature Coefficients ($\alpha$ values)}

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Temperature coefficients used for correction.}
\label{tab:alpha}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lccc@{}}
\toprule
Material & $\alpha$ (\%/\si{\degreeCelsius}) & $\alpha$ (fractional) & Source \\
\midrule
N42EH   & $-0.10$ & $-0.0010$ & Allstar Magnetics datasheet \\
N52SH   & $-0.11$ & $-0.0011$ & Allstar Magnetics datasheet \\
SmCo33H & $-0.04$ & $-0.0004$ & Allstar Magnetics datasheet \\
SmCo35  & $-0.04$ & $-0.0004$ & Allstar Magnetics datasheet \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Note: NdFeB magnets are about 2.5$\times$ more temperature-sensitive than SmCo
magnets. This asymmetry has important consequences (see below).

\subsection{Worked Example}

Suppose you measure Y-17-1 (an N42EH magnet) in the Helmholtz coil and get:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Raw reading: \SI{0.9234}{mWC}
  \item Teslameter temperature: \SI{27.5}{\degreeCelsius}
\end{itemize}

Correction:
\begin{align*}
\alpha &= -0.0010 \quad \text{(for N42EH)} \\
T_{\mathrm{measured}} &= 27.5 \\
T_{\mathrm{ref}} &= 20.0 \\[6pt]
H_{\mathrm{corrected}} &= \frac{0.9234}{1 + (-0.0010)(27.5 - 20.0)} \\
  &= \frac{0.9234}{1 + (-0.0010)(7.5)} \\
  &= \frac{0.9234}{1 - 0.0075} \\
  &= \frac{0.9234}{0.9925} \\
  &= \SI{0.93037}{mWC}
\end{align*}

The corrected value is higher than the raw value because the magnet was warm
(above \SI{20}{\degreeCelsius}), so its raw reading was artificially low.

\subsection{Why Temperature Matters for the Differential}

Even though the differential compares NdFeB to SmCo on the same plate (same
temperature), the temperature correction does NOT perfectly cancel because the
$\alpha$ values are different:

\begin{align*}
\text{Differential temperature sensitivity}
  &= \bar{\alpha}_{\mathrm{NdFeB}} - \alpha_{\mathrm{SmCo}} \\
  &= \frac{(-0.10) + (-0.11)}{2} - (-0.04) \\
  &= -0.105 - (-0.04) \\
  &= -0.065\;\text{\%/\si{\degreeCelsius}}
\end{align*}

So for every \SI{1}{\degreeCelsius} error in our baseline temperature estimate,
the differential shifts by 0.065\% (we round to 0.066\%/\si{\degreeCelsius}
accounting for the exact weighted average). This is the single largest remaining
systematic: a $\pm$\SI{0.5}{\degreeCelsius} temperature uncertainty produces
$\pm 0.033\%$ uncertainty on the differential.

\section{The Intra-Plate Differential}
\label{sec:differential}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{The Problem: Gain Drift}

The Helmholtz coil + fluxmeter system drifts by 0.5--0.7\% between measurement
sessions. This is a multiplicative effect: every reading in a given session is
scaled by the same gain factor $G$.

If the gain on the day you took the baseline was $G_{\mathrm{pre}} = 1.003$,
and the gain on the day you took the post-deployment measurement was
$G_{\mathrm{post}} = 0.997$, then every magnet appears to have changed by about
$(0.997 - 1.003)/1.003 = -0.6\%$, even if nothing physical happened. This is
much larger than our ${\sim}0.2\%$ signal.

\subsection{The Solution: Measure 4 Materials Together}

Each Y-plate carries one magnet of each of the 4 grades. All 4 are measured in
the same session, typically within ${\sim}$2 minutes of each other. The
percentage change for each magnet is:

\begin{equation*}
\Delta_i = \frac{G_{\mathrm{post}}\,H_{i,\mathrm{post}} - G_{\mathrm{pre}}\,H_{i,\mathrm{pre}}}
                {G_{\mathrm{pre}}\,H_{i,\mathrm{pre}}} \times 100\%
\end{equation*}

When we take the difference between the NdFeB average and the SmCo average:

\begin{equation*}
\delta = \frac{\Delta_{\mathrm{N42EH}} + \Delta_{\mathrm{N52SH}}}{2}
       - \frac{\Delta_{\mathrm{SmCo33H}} + \Delta_{\mathrm{SmCo35}}}{2}
\end{equation*}

The gain factors cancel to first order because:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item All 4 magnets in the baseline share the same $G_{\mathrm{pre}}$
  \item All 4 magnets in the post-deployment share the same $G_{\mathrm{post}}$
  \item The gain affects all readings as a common multiplicative factor
  \item The difference removes the common-mode shift
\end{enumerate}

This is why we call it the ``gain-immune'' or ``intra-plate'' differential. It
removes the $\pm 0.124\%$ gain systematic entirely, improving precision by a
factor of ${\sim}4.4$.

\subsection{Worked Example}

Suppose Y-17 has these readings (temperature-corrected to \SI{20}{\degreeCelsius}):

Percentage changes:
\begin{align*}

\text{SmCo33H:} &\quad (1.0346 - 1.0345)\,/\,1.0345 \times 100 = +0.010\% \\
\text{N52SH:}   &\quad (0.9797 - 0.9812)\,/\,0.9812 \times 100 = -0.153\% \\
\text{SmCo35:}  &\quad (1.0752 - 1.0756)\,/\,1.0756 \times 100 = -0.037\%
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
\text{NdFeB mean:}   &\quad (-0.249 + (-0.153))\,/\,2 = -0.201\% \\
\text{SmCo mean:}    &\quad (+0.010 + (-0.037))\,/\,2 = -0.014\% \\
\text{Differential:} &\quad -0.201 - (-0.014) = -0.187\%
\end{align*}

This plate shows NdFeB degraded ${\sim}0.19\%$ more than SmCo. We compute this
for all 30 tunnel plates and take the fleet mean.

\subsection{Why This Works Even With Gain Drift}

Imagine the gain was 0.5\% high during the baseline and 0.3\% low during the
post-deployment measurement. Both NdFeB and SmCo readings shift by the same
${\sim}0.8\%$ (the difference in gains). When you subtract SmCo from NdFeB, the
0.8\% cancels. The only thing that survives is the real physical difference
between the materials.

\textbf{Key requirement:} All 4 slots must be measured in the same session,
which our protocol ensures. If you accidentally mix slots from different sessions
on the same plate, the differential is corrupted.


%=============================================================================
\section{Gain Systematic Quantification}
\label{sec:gain}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{How We Measured the Gain Drift}

We had 5 lab measurement sessions (April--June 2025) where every lab plate was
measured multiple times, compared against a pre-deployment baseline taken in
November 2024. Since lab plates were never exposed to tunnel radiation, any
variation between these sessions and the baseline is purely instrumental (gain
drift + noise).

\subsection{The Half-Range Method}

For each magnet sample across the 5 sessions:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item Compute the percent change between each session and the mean baseline
  \item Find the maximum and minimum percent change across sessions
  \item The half-range $= (\max - \min)\,/\,2$
\end{enumerate}

Then average over all samples to get the fleet-wide gain systematic.

\subsection{Cleaned vs Uncleaned}

Two known outlier samples (Y-34-4 and Y-40-4) showed $>3\%$ variations due to
suspected baseline measurement errors. Including them inflates the systematic:

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Gain systematic: cleaned vs uncleaned.}
\label{tab:gain-clean}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcc@{}}
\toprule
Version & Gain systematic & Outlier handling \\
\midrule
\textbf{Cleaned} & $\pm 0.124\%$ & Y-34-4 and Y-40-4 excluded \\
Uncleaned        & $\pm 0.248\%$ & All samples included \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

We report the cleaned value ($\pm 0.124\%$) as the primary gain systematic, with
the uncleaned value as a conservative alternative.

\subsection{Impact on Results}

\begin{table}[htbp]

This is why the differential is so powerful: it turns a $\pm 0.124\%$ systematic

%=============================================================================
\section{Within-Session Drift}
\label{sec:wsd}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{The Question}

We established that session-to-session gain drift cancels in the intra-plate
differential. But what about drift \emph{within} a session? If the Helmholtz
coil gain drifts while you are measuring a single plate's 4 slots (over
${\sim}$2 minutes), this could contaminate the differential.

\subsection{Analysis 1: Slot Timing}

From second-precision timestamps in each data file, we measured the time between
the first and last slot measurement for every plate:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Median gap:} \SI{102}{\second} (\SI{1.70}{min})
  \item \textbf{95th percentile:} \SI{161}{\second} (\SI{2.69}{min})
  \item \textbf{Worst case:} Y-6 on January 8, \SI{6.6}{min} (only 3 valid slots)
\end{itemize}

At a hypothetical drift rate of 1\%/hour:

\begin{equation*}
\text{Drift in \SI{102}{\second}} = 1\%/\text{hr} \times (102/3600)\;\text{hr} = 0.028\%
\end{equation*}

But the drift would be common-mode across slots (same direction), so the
residual on the NdFeB--SmCo differential would be even smaller (the drift is
partially randomized by the slot order on each plate).

\section{Baseline Temperature}
\label{sec:baseline-temp}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{The Problem}

Before the Teslameter was consistently co-located with the Helmholtz
measurements, baseline temperatures were uncertain. The pre-deployment
measurements (September--November 2024) used the Teslameter probe, which reads
about \SI{0.8}{\degreeCelsius} higher than the estimated ambient temperature.
This offset could reflect probe self-heating, or it could indicate that the
sample surface was genuinely warmer than the room air (the probe is in thermal
contact with the magnet).

\subsection{The v1/v2/v3 Progression}

Our temperature correction evolved through three versions as we understood the
temperature data better:

\textbf{Version 1 (v1): Raw probe temperatures}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Used Teslameter temperatures as-is (24.6--\SI{26.7}{\degreeCelsius})
  \item Result: differential $= -0.266\% \pm 0.028\%$ ($9.6\sigma$)
  \item Problem: Probe biased ${\sim}$\SI{0.8}{\degreeCelsius} high, overcorrects NdFeB more than SmCo
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Version 2 (v2): Blanket \SI{23}{\degreeCelsius} correction}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Assumed all baselines were at \SI{23}{\degreeCelsius}
  \item Result: differential $= -0.134\% \pm 0.028\%$ ($4.9\sigma$)
  \item Problem: SmCo33H anomalously positive ($+0.083\%$, $2.7\sigma$)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Version 3 (v3, current): Per-date calibrated estimates}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Used same-day Teslameter H-plate readings to calibrate Y-plate probe bias
  \item Baseline temperatures per session estimated at 23.8--\SI{25.8}{\degreeCelsius}
  \item Result: differential $= -0.208\% \pm 0.028\%$ ($7.6\sigma$)
  \item SmCo anomaly resolved; SmCo33H now $+0.037\%$ ($1.2\sigma$, consistent with zero)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The $\pm$\SI{0.5}{\degreeCelsius} Uncertainty}

Our best estimate of the baseline temperature uncertainty is
$\pm$\SI{0.5}{\degreeCelsius}, based on:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item Two same-day Y--H measurement cross-checks showing $+0.77$ and
    $+$\SI{0.87}{\degreeCelsius} probe bias (mean $+$\SI{0.82}{\degreeCelsius},
    spread ${\sim}$\SI{0.1}{\degreeCelsius})
  \item Day-to-day lab temperature variation of ${\sim}$\SI{3}{\degreeCelsius}
    across November 2024
  \item Per-date corrections where direct H-plate readings exist
\end{enumerate}

Impact on the differential:
\begin{equation*}
0.066\%/\si{\degreeCelsius} \times \SI{0.5}{\degreeCelsius} = 0.033\%
\end{equation*}

This $\pm 0.033\%$ is the single largest systematic in the error budget.

\subsection{Robustness: Temperature Scan}

Even scanning the assumed baseline temperature over its full plausible range

\subsection{What the New Arduino Sensor Changes}

The AM2302 provides an independent ambient air temperature measurement at the
time of each Helmholtz reading. Having two independent temperature data streams
(Arduino ambient + Teslameter probe) is strictly better than having one, because:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item If both sensors agree, confidence in the temperature is high.
  \item If they disagree, the disagreement itself is diagnostic (e.g., the sample
    may not have equilibrated to room temperature).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Open question:} The temperature correction formula needs the sample
temperature, not the air temperature. The Teslameter probe, being in contact
with the sample, may be closer to the truth when the sample and air temperatures
differ (e.g., early morning when samples are still cool from overnight). The
Arduino, being an ambient air sensor, could be biased by drafts, laptop fans,
HVAC flow, or other local air currents. Conversely, the Teslameter probe may
have a self-heating bias. Determining which sensor (or what combination) gives
the most accurate temperature for the correction is a primary goal of the summer
cross-calibration campaign. Do not assume either sensor is ``better'' until this
work is complete.

\section{Teslameter Positioning Noise}
\label{sec:positioning}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{The Problem}

The Teslameter measures the magnetic field at a single point on the magnet
surface. If the magnet sits slightly differently in the 3D-printed rig each time
you insert it, the probe location relative to the magnet changes, and the field
reading changes. This is ``positioning noise.''

\subsection{Per-Face Precision}

From Campaign~1 data (repeated measurements of the same samples):

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Teslameter per-face standard deviation.}
\label{tab:positioning}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lcp{7cm}@{}}
\toprule
Face & Std & Notes \\
\midrule
\textbf{Top}   & 0.14--0.29\% & Best precision; least sensitive to alignment \\
\textbf{Front} & 0.57--0.91\% & Moderate; field gradient steeper \\
\textbf{Side}  & 0.90--1.90\% & Worst; highest field gradient \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textbf{Why top is best:} The top face has the flattest field gradient (the
field changes slowly with position), so a \SI{0.1}{\milli\meter} shift produces
less change in the reading.

\subsection{H-Plate First-Tunnel Bias}

The first time H-plates were measured in the tunnel (July 17, 2025), a
systematic bias appeared:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item 62\% of front-face readings were $>$2\% low compared to subsequent measurements
  \item 61\% of side-face readings were $>$2\% low
  \item Top face was unaffected
\end{itemize}

This is consistent with a different rig setup or calibration on the first tunnel
trip. It does not affect the Helmholtz results (which do not use the Teslameter
for demagnetization measurement).

\subsection{Impact on Helmholtz Results}

The positioning noise does NOT affect Helmholtz readings (the Helmholtz
measurement integrates over the full sample volume and is position-independent).
The Teslameter is used only for temperature in the Helmholtz analysis pipeline.

\subsection{Impact on the Teslameter as an Independent Science Measurement}

This is where positioning noise matters enormously. If the Teslameter noise can
be controlled (especially on the top face, where it is already 0.14--0.29\%),
then Teslameter data becomes a primary science measurement in its own right, not
just a cross-check. The scientific value of showing both:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Integrated moment loss} (Helmholtz, volume-averaged) AND
  \item \textbf{Spatially-resolved point-field loss} (Teslameter, per-face, per-axis)
\end{itemize}

\noindent is much greater than either alone. A paper that demonstrates consistent
demagnetization signatures across two independent measurement techniques, on
multiple sample geometries (Y-plates, H-plates, A-samples), is qualitatively
stronger than Helmholtz-only results.

For H-plates in particular, the Teslameter measures field in a Halbach-like
configuration, which is the actual geometry relevant to FFA magnets. Showing
point-field degradation in Halbach geometry directly addresses the design
question.

\subsection{Improvement Opportunity}

The 3D-printed rigs have inherent backlash and positioning tolerance. A machined
metal jig for top-face measurements could potentially reduce the 0.14--0.29\%
noise floor. This is a high-priority target: getting top-face noise below
${\sim}0.15\%$ would make the Teslameter precise enough to independently detect
the ${\sim}0.2\%$ demagnetization signal, transforming it from a noisy
cross-check into a publishable primary measurement.

\section{Statistical Methods}
\label{sec:stats}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Basic Statistics}

\textbf{Mean:} The average of $N$ values.
\begin{equation*}
\bar{x} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} x_i
\end{equation*}

\textbf{Standard deviation (sample):} A measure of spread. We use
\texttt{ddof=1} (Bessel's correction) because we are estimating from a sample:
\begin{equation*}
s = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1} \sum_{i=1}^{N} (x_i - \bar{x})^2}
\end{equation*}

In Python: \texttt{np.std(data, ddof=1)}

\textbf{Standard error of the mean (SEM):} How precisely we know the mean:
\begin{equation*}
\mathrm{SEM} = \frac{s}{\sqrt{N}}
\end{equation*}

With 30 tunnel plates and $\mathrm{std} \approx 0.15\%$, the SEM is
$0.15/\sqrt{30} = 0.028\%$.

\subsection{Percentage Change Formula}

For each sample:
\begin{equation*}
\text{pct\_change} = \frac{\text{latest\_corrected} - \text{baseline\_mean\_corrected}}
                          {\text{baseline\_mean\_corrected}} \times 100
\end{equation*}

where ``corrected'' means temperature-corrected to \SI{20}{\degreeCelsius}.

\subsection{Per-Plate Differential}

For each Y-plate with slots assigned to materials:
\begin{equation*}
\delta = \overline{\Delta}_{\mathrm{NdFeB}} - \overline{\Delta}_{\mathrm{SmCo}}
\end{equation*}

The fleet differential is the mean of $\delta$ across all tunnel plates.

\subsection{Sigma Significance}

How many standard errors the result is from zero:
\begin{equation*}
\sigma = \frac{|\text{result}|}{\text{uncertainty}}
\end{equation*}

For example:
\begin{align*}
\text{differential} &= -0.208\% \\
\mathrm{SEM} &= 0.028\% \\
\sigma &= 0.208\,/\,0.028 = 7.4 \quad \text{(approximately 7.6 with exact numbers)}
\end{align*}

Rough guide:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item $1\sigma$: 68\% confidence, not significant
  \item $2\sigma$: 95\% confidence, suggestive
  \item $3\sigma$: 99.7\% confidence, evidence
  \item $5\sigma$: 1 in 3.5 million chance of noise, discovery-level
\end{itemize}

Our $7.6\sigma$ is extremely significant statistically.

\subsection{Combined Significance (Quadrature)}

When multiple independent uncertainties contribute, combine them in quadrature:
\begin{equation*}
\sigma_{\mathrm{combined}} = \sqrt{\sigma_{\mathrm{stat}}^2 + \sigma_{\mathrm{syst,1}}^2 + \sigma_{\mathrm{syst,2}}^2 + \cdots}
\end{equation*}

For our differential:
\begin{align*}
\sigma_{\mathrm{combined}} &= \sqrt{0.028^2 + 0.033^2 + 0.014^2} = 0.045\% \\
\text{combined significance} &= 0.208\,/\,0.045 = 4.6\sigma
\end{align*}

\subsection{When to Use Paired vs Absolute Tests}

\textbf{Paired (preferred for Y-plates):} Each plate has its own NdFeB--SmCo
differential. Compare the distribution of these differentials to zero. This is
what we do for the headline result.

\textbf{Absolute (for individual materials):} Compare the mean percent change
for all N42EH samples to zero. This carries the full gain systematic.

\textbf{Unpaired (for tunnel vs lab):} Compare the mean differential from 30
tunnel plates to the mean from 9 lab plates. Different plates, so unpaired.
Combined SEM $= \sqrt{\mathrm{SEM}_{\mathrm{tunnel}}^2 + \mathrm{SEM}_{\mathrm{lab}}^2}$.

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Why 9 Lab Plates Is Enough}

With 9 plates and a per-plate differential std of ${\sim}0.11\%$, the lab SEM is
$0.11/\sqrt{9} = 0.037\%$, giving us ${\sim}3\times$ resolution on any
systematic bias at the 0.1\% level. A null result at 0.038\% SEM means any real
lab effect is smaller than ${\sim}0.08\%$ ($2\sigma$ upper bound).

\subsection{What This Rules Out}

The lab null result rules out:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Systematic bias in the temperature correction
  \item Material-dependent aging in the absence of radiation (at least at our
    measurement precision, over this timeframe)
  \item Helmholtz coil response depending on material type
  \item Any analysis pipeline bug that produces a NdFeB--SmCo difference
\end{itemize}
