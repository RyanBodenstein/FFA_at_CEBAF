================================================================================
WORST-CASE THERMAL AGING CALCULATION
================================================================================

Context: Testing whether thermal aging at tunnel temperatures can explain
the observed -0.208% +/- 0.028% NdFeB-SmCo differential from Campaign 1.

Reference: emagnetsUK vendor curve, 100C, generic NdFeB (likely N35-N42)
Worst-case: 45C for full 12 months (8,760 hours)
Note: 45C was a single outlier reading; typical tunnel temp is 30-35C.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PART 1: Arrhenius Scaling from 100C to 45C (Generic NdFeB, No Grade Correction)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The thermal aging rate follows Arrhenius: rate ~ exp(-Ea / kT)
12 months at 45C is equivalent to X hours at 100C, where X depends on Ea.

 Ea (eV)    Scale factor    Equiv 100C hrs  Generic loss (%)
----------------------------------------------------------
    0.50        0.068003             595.7            1.7351
    0.70        0.023203             203.3            1.7005
    1.00        0.004624              40.5            1.6108
    1.25        0.001206              10.6            1.5247
    1.50        0.000314               2.8            1.3524

Interpretation:
  Ea = 0.5 eV is unrealistically low (gives ~600 equiv hours, well into
  the logarithmic regime of the 100C curve).
  Ea = 1.0-1.5 eV is typical for NdFeB domain relaxation.
  At Ea = 1.0 eV, 12 months at 45C = 40 hours at 100C for the GENERIC grade.

PART 5: The Dysprosium Inversion Test
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

If thermal aging drives the signal, the grade with LOWER coercivity
and LOWER max operating temperature should age MORE:

  Thermal aging prediction:
    N42EH (Hci=30, max 190C): LESS aging
    N52SH (Hci=19, max 140C): MORE aging

  Observation:
    N42EH: -0.252 +/- 0.036%  (MORE degradation!)
    N52SH: -0.170 +/- 0.036%  (LESS degradation)
    Difference: 0.082% in the WRONG direction for thermal aging

  Radiation hypothesis (Dy neutron capture):
    N42EH contains 1-2% Dy (sigma = 994 barn)
    N52SH contains 0% Dy
    Dy captures neutrons -> local lattice damage -> explains inversion

  This inversion CANNOT be explained by thermal aging regardless of
  the assumed activation energy or grade correction model.

PART 7b: Tunnel-vs-Lab Differential Aging (Critical Refinement)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Our measurement compares tunnel plates (at elevated temp) to lab plates
(at room temp). BOTH sets undergo the initial knock-down. The relevant
quantity is not the absolute aging but the EXCESS aging at tunnel temp
vs lab temp.

Pre-measurement was done before deployment. Then:
  - Tunnel magnets: sit at ~45C for 12 months
  - Lab magnets: sit at ~21C for 12 months

Tunnel NdFeB-SmCo diff captures NdFeB aging at tunnel temp (SmCo ~ 0).
Lab NdFeB-SmCo diff captures NdFeB aging at lab temp (SmCo ~ 0).
Tunnel-Lab difference = EXCESS aging from elevated temperature.

          Grade  Aging @45C (%)  Aging @21C (%)      Excess (%)
--------------------------------------------------------------
  Generic NdFeB          1.6108          1.3053          0.3055
          N42EH          0.0910          0.0738          0.0173
          N52SH          0.2405          0.1949          0.0456

NdFeB avg excess aging (45C vs 21C):    0.0314%
Observed tunnel-lab differential:       -0.201%  (= -0.208 - (-0.007))
Ratio (observed / predicted excess):    6.4x

CRITICAL FINDING: The predicted excess aging is 0.0314%,
6x too small to explain the observed 0.201% tunnel-lab difference.

Furthermore, the model predicts that lab NdFeB should ALSO show aging
(0.074% for N42EH, 0.195% for N52SH at 21C). But the observed lab
NdFeB-SmCo differential is -0.007 +/- 0.038%, consistent with zero.
This means the grade correction is actually TOO CONSERVATIVE, and the
true aging at these temperatures is even smaller than predicted here.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PART 9: The Haavisto T_0 Framework
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Haavisto & Paju (2009, IEEE Trans. Magn. 45(12), 5277-5280) established
that sintered NdFeB with a square J-H curve is thermally stable below a
critical temperature T_0, above which irreversible losses suddenly appear.
T_0 depends on Hci and the permeance coefficient (Pc).

For high-coercivity material (Hci ~25-30 kOe), T_0 is approximately
120-150C for typical geometries (Pc > 1).

Haavisto et al. (2014, Adv. Mater. Sci. Eng. 2014, 760584) showed that
the estimated flux loss after 30 YEARS is approximately 2x the loss
measured after just 1 HOUR. This is a consequence of the logarithmic
time dependence: delta_J = S * ln(t/t0).

Application to our magnets:

       Grade    Hci(20C)   T_operating       T_0 (estimated)
----------------------------------------------------------
       N42EH        30 kOe          45 C               ~150C
       N52SH        19 kOe          45 C           ~100-120C
     SmCo33H        25 kOe          45 C              >>200C
      SmCo35        18 kOe          45 C              >>200C

All our grades operate far below their respective T_0 values.
According to the Haavisto framework, time-dependent flux losses
below T_0 are negligible even on multi-decade timescales.

Haavisto et al. (2013, EPJ Web Conf. 40, 06001) further showed that
magnets with square J-H curves (characteristic of high-Hci grades)
do NOT benefit from pre-stabilization treatment, because they are
already stable below T_0. This suggests that even if our magnets were
not pre-stabilized, the initial knock-down for EH/SH grades at 45C
would be negligible.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PART 10: Sensitivity to Hci Correction Exponent
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The Hci^-n correction in Parts 3-4 uses n=2. This exponent is motivated
by the Stoner-Wohlfarth model for coherent rotation, where the energy
barrier scales as E_b ~ K*V*(1-H/Hci)^2 (Kronmuller & Fahnle, 2003;
Givord et al., 1988, IEEE Trans. Magn. 24(2), 1921-1923). For
nucleation-type reversal (the dominant mechanism in sintered NdFeB),
the exponent is >= 2. The physical scaling is actually exponential
through the Boltzmann factor: rate ~ exp(-Hci*V_act/kT).

Sensitivity of the excess aging (tunnel-vs-lab) to the exponent:

  Exponent   N42EH (%)   N52SH (%)    Avg Excess   Shortfall
-------------------------------------------------------
       1.0     0.0726%     0.1181%       0.0953%        2.1x
       1.5     0.0354%     0.0734%       0.0544%        3.7x
       2.0     0.0173%     0.0456%       0.0314%        6.4x
       3.0     0.0041%     0.0176%       0.0109%       18.5x

Even with the most favorable exponent (n=1, linear scaling), the
excess aging shortfall is still substantial. With the physically
motivated n=2, the shortfall is 6x. With n=3 (more realistic for
nucleation), the shortfall grows further.

Note: the true physical scaling (exponential) would give correction
factors orders of magnitude smaller than any power-law model,
making the shortfall even larger.

================================================================================
SUMMARY
================================================================================

1. QUANTITATIVE: Under worst-case assumptions (45C, 12 months, Ea=1.0 eV,
   conservative Hci^-2 grade correction):
   - Absolute NdFeB aging at 45C: ~0.166% (vs observed -0.208%)
   - But the proper comparison is tunnel-vs-lab EXCESS aging: ~0.0314%
   - This is 6x too small to explain the observed 0.201% tunnel-lab
     difference. Both tunnel and lab magnets undergo the initial knock-down;
     only the slow creep rate differs between temperatures.

2. HAAVISTO T_0: Peer-reviewed aging studies (Haavisto & Paju, 2009) show
   that high-Hci NdFeB is stable below a critical temperature T_0 (~120-
   150C for our grades). At 45C, our magnets are far below T_0, where
   time-dependent losses are negligible even on multi-decade timescales.

3. QUALITATIVE: The Dy inversion (N42EH > N52SH) is strongly suggestive
   of radiation damage (1.6 sigma, with quantitative Dy neutron capture
   explanation) and runs counter to any thermal aging prediction.

4. CONTROLS: Lab plates at 21C show no NdFeB-SmCo differential (-0.007 +/-
   0.038%), consistent with negligible aging at room temperature.

5. DOSE CORRELATION: Degradation correlates with neutron dose (rho=0.389,
   p=0.03) but not gamma dose (rho=0.21, p=0.27). Temperature does not
   selectively produce neutron-correlated effects.

6. COOL ZONES: Labyrinth plates (near ambient, N=2) show NdFeB-specific
   degradation, consistent with scattered radiation reaching those
   locations (confirmed by rod dosimetry).

CONCLUSION: Thermal aging alone is unlikely to explain the observed signal.
  - Quantitatively: the tunnel-vs-lab excess aging is 6x too small
  - Peer-reviewed Haavisto T_0 framework confirms our magnets operate
    far below the threshold for significant time-dependent flux loss
  - Multiple converging lines of evidence (Dy inversion, neutron
    correlation, material specificity) point to radiation, not temperature
  - Campaign 2 data will provide independent confirmation
